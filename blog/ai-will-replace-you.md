## My experience with AI

Over the past few years, I can barely open LinkedIn without running into some post about "AI will replace you." Every week brings a new tool, a new model version, a new benchmark surpassing the last one. At work, I have already sat through meetings dedicated entirely to learning how to use AI in our daily workflow.

A lot is happening all at once. And, being honest: it scared me. Software Engineering is changing at a pace no previous generation of developers has ever faced.

## The changes in software engineering

But let us get straight to the point: **no, AI will not replace us**. It will, however, completely redefine our role.

Think about what happened with compilers. When they appeared, programming in Assembly was an essential skill. It was the only way to "talk" to the machine. The compiler abstracted that layer away. Today, most engineers have never written a single line of Assembly, and that is fine. The profession did not disappear; it moved up one level of abstraction.

With AI, we are living through that same shift. The "raw code" layer, such as repetitive functions, boilerplate, and well-known patterns, will increasingly be generated by language models. The software engineer will be responsible for what compilers never could handle:

> Understanding the user's real problem. Defining the architecture. Ensuring security. Reviewing what AI produced with critical judgment. And, above all, asking the right questions.

Those who understand **how** code works, and not just **how to ask** AI to generate it, will have a significant advantage. Because only those who understand the mechanism can identify when the output is wrong.

## How AI works: non-determinism

One of the things that helped me most in letting go of that fear was understanding how AI actually works, and more importantly, what it **cannot** do.

Language models like GPT or Claude are **non-deterministic** systems: given the same input, they can produce different outputs on each run. This happens because the generation process involves probabilistic sampling. The model does not "calculate" the correct answer; it assigns probabilities to each next token and samples from the most likely ones, with a degree of randomness controlled by a parameter called **temperature**.

This has enormous practical implications for those working in software:

**AI does not guarantee correctness.** A model can generate code that looks plausible, compiles without errors, and still contains a subtle logic bug. It does not "know" it is wrong; it simply produced the most likely text given the context. Human review is not optional; it is structural.

**Hallucinations are a byproduct of the design.** When a model invents a library that does not exist or cites a fictional RFC, it is not "lying." It is being faithful to what its mechanism does: generating tokens that are coherent with learned patterns, even without access to reality. An engineer who understands this knows exactly when to trust and when to verify.

**Context is everything.** Output quality depends directly on input quality. Knowing how to craft a well-structured prompt, with enough context and clear constraints, is a technical skill. It is no different from writing a good specification.

## What this means for me

I am in my master's degree studying exactly the layers that AI still does not dominate: systems, security, and software architecture. The more I learn, the more I realize that the initial fear was a useful signal. It forced me to look at what truly matters to master.

AI is an extraordinary tool. But extraordinary tools have always required extraordinary operators. The pneumatic hammer did not replace the architect.
